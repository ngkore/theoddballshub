By the time you have an acceptance letter in hand, it's easy to feel like the hard part
is over. It isn't — the embassy appointment is its own gate, and a fair number of
applicants get rejected here simply because they show up underprepared. This is what we
learned preparing for one, at the Czech embassy in New Delhi. The exact document list
can vary slightly by embassy and nationality, so treat this as a strong starting point,
not gospel.

## Before the appointment

- Your appointment date and time is usually **fixed and cannot be changed** — plan
  travel, work, everything else around it, not the other way round.
- Bring **two sets of every document** — originals and photocopies.
- Documents generally need to be **dated within the last 180 days**.

## The core document list

- Long-term visa / long-term residence permit application form
- Copy of your passport
- Acceptance letter — usually needs to be **in Czech** (or accompanied by a certified
  translation)
- Proof of accommodation — also typically required **in Czech**
- A stamped and signed **bank statement**, plus a copy of the international debit card
  in your name issued by the same bank
- A **police clearance certificate**, with a certified translation into Czech done by
  a translator officially recognised by the Czech Ministry of Justice (there's a public
  register of approved translators — check it well in advance, good translators get
  booked up)
- If you've lived in **any other country for more than 6 months in the last 3 years**,
  you'll also need a police clearance certificate (summary) covering that period —
  don't forget this if you studied or worked abroad before this move

## A note for Indian applicants specifically

If you're applying from India, the police clearance certificate needs to come from
your **Regional Passport Office** *and* be **apostilled** before you submit it. Start
this early — apostille turnaround can be a bottleneck on its own, separate from the
degree-recognition apostille you may already be juggling.

(Applicants from Sri Lanka, Maldives or Bhutan have a different requirement — the
certificate needs to be legalised at the Consular Department of your country's Ministry
of Foreign Affairs instead. If that's you, prioritise this step similarly.)

## Preparing for the interview itself

The embassy is assessing whether you're a genuine, motivated student — not just
checking boxes. Go in able to talk about:

- The **official name of your university** in both Czech and English (these can
  differ meaningfully — know both).
- Basic facts about your **faculty and programme**: how long it takes, how it's
  structured (semesters, any final thesis/exam), and why you chose it.
- That your courses are taught in **English**, and ideally show an English
  certificate (IELTS/TOEFL/TOEIC) if you have one — even if your programme's own
  entrance exam already assessed your English.
- How you handled **degree recognition** (Nostrification or Internal Evaluation) — they
  may ask.
- A few basic facts about the **Czech Republic and the EU** generally.

## What we'd tell a friend

Don't walk in having only skimmed your acceptance letter. Spend an evening actually
reading your programme's page, know the two names of your university, and get your
documents translated and apostilled well before the appointment date — there's no
"come back next week" if something's missing or out of date.

*Based on our experience with one appointment in New Delhi — exact requirements vary by
embassy, nationality and current rules, so confirm directly with your embassy before
your appointment.*
